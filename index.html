UX 1400

trevorsuarez.me Design Rationale

This design needed to be simple, as there is not lots of content to be displayed. Starting with that mission, I built the site, so it was easy to navigate (single page) and added a bright orange to reflect my characteristics in the color theory. I mostly corelate with friendly, cheerful, confidence. The orange/mango color happens to be the same color that I wanted my HydroFlask to be. On the main screen, I chose to make my name larger to help draw the visitor in to read the small bio about me. I also made sure to include social media links because this is a professional portfolio, which means they are looking at my work. I knew if I would add a giant picture or video, the attention would be on the picture/video and thus not on my info and experiences. In terms of the one page, I believe staying on one page is necessary to simplify how a user interacts. No need for back and forward buttons because the navbar is always present and scrolling works too. I chose to put the navigation bar on the left side because vertical space is always limited, but many screens have horizontal space. That way, I can maximize the vertical space on each page. Visitors of the site are interested in learning about me and my experiences/skills. Therefore, I highlight each internship/job I have had in their own dedicated paragraph with individual top headers. Having skills/tools as another highlight with logos helps people get a quick overview without having to read, thus saving time while getting the message at the same time. Overall, I hope the simple design attracts professionals and helps edge myself out amongst the competition. Without a doubt, the motivation March discuss that I designed for is status, as discussed in Marsh. As a SE background, I cannot have websites that are created using a drag and drop builder. It’s a disgrace to the software engineering community. With that being said, I wanted to create a project that I can call mine as an example of my skills all wrapped into one. With my software engineering a user experience knowledge, I believe this is a great representation of two of my interests combined. For the visitors of this site, I want them to see it and take away a little small piece of me when they navigate to another site. I’m not trying to be popular by having a site, I simply want to have a bold presence for those who do make a visit. By choosing mango as the main color, I wanted to communicate my energy and warmth as if the person was meeting me.

UX 1400 Reflection

I learned that UX is more than just making things pretty and easy to read on a screen, it is way more than that. Let me explain. On the first day of class, we had to all answer “What is UX?” This perplexed me, because this question challenged my knowledge of UX. The motivation in myself that I discovered on the first day of class propelled me to learning a wide range of topics including design tactics and motivations. My biggest takeaway was that UX concepts can be used in more than one way depending on the scenario at hand. I assumed that there were always standards that should be met, and that’s it. Reading from Marsh’s book and hearing from UX professionals, I have realized that everyone has their own style, but in a way that still mimics UX to the core. I am also grateful for the opportunity to integrate my iterative design thinking into the class curriculum. This was another way I could get used to working with others and opening my perspective to others who aren’t familiar to iterative design thinking. This course related to my career aspirations because I am at a crossroad in my SE path. I don’t want to be a 24/7 developer, but I am not sure what I do want to be. This is my last class in the UX minor path and without this class, I wouldn’t be currently considering UX as a possible path of mine. The more I use my skills in my SE work such as designing UIs or creating brands, the more I realize that UX could be my path. As a result, this class has changed me, but it will take time to see how great of an influence it had on my path.

Projects

Discovery World

When the Discovery World project was first introduced, my initial thought was “they want me to go discover what other people are discovering at Discovery World.” I was astonished, but in the end, I did learn a thing or two about myself and how I analyze environments. To provide a little background, Discovery World is a museum/hands-on interactive learning environment located on the shoreline of Lake Michigan downtown Milwaukee, Wisconsin. My group chose to take a deeper look into Automation Everywhere by Rockwell Automation. The exhibit explores how industrial robots don’t necessarily have to be used for factory floors, but instead playing games showing off their smarts and technology. I was tasked with being a researcher which included visiting discovery world, analyzing the exhibit, and coming up with ratings for the robots. The final deliverable was an analysis of the current exhibit and improvements that we believe could be made to the interactions between the robots and exhibit visitors. We had roughly three weeks to complete the project.

Reflection

The team learned that there are some rough user experiences in the exhibit. We did a great job identifying the brand strength and did a great job with the descriptions of how things can improve. I gained hands on, real world experience, something that I didn’t receive when working on my own projects. For the next project, I would focus on spending more time as a team coming to consensus, as I feel everyone was on slightly different pages. This was something that I worked on for the next project, Tricklebee Café.

Tricklebee Cafe

For the Tricklebee Café project, I was given the opportunity to help build the curriculum for the project. I was able to bring to the table my experiences with iterative design thinking and creative problem solving to help build the daily missions for the class. For the project, my team decided to target our pitch towards farmers in southeastern Wisconsin. The project only lasted one week. I was personally tasked with organizing the group and build out the pitch format. I also helped conduct the brainstorming sessions as I have experience iterating through creative problems such as this one. As a group, we were constrained to creating awareness for Tricklebee Cafe. Although that might seem simple, it was very complex and required lots of attention to the details. Creating awareness can come in many shapes and forms. The details lied in the story of our persona and how other personas (farmers) would me interacting with the minimum viable product (MVP).

Reflection

The team decided to target farmers because they are the source of the food. The farmers bring the food to many places such as grocery stores and farmer’s markets. This means that they have food in bulk and possibly other food that they cannot sell. This is where the project’s MVP begins. For the pitch, the team told the story of Greg, who is a farmer in Southeastern Wisconsin. He is a community man, who appreciates giving back. He first saw an ad for Trickebee Cafe, then got talking to the owner, then decided to contribute. It was the relationship that he built with the owner that drone his motivation. There are more farmers like Greg, who step to the plate when a meaningful connection is made. This project helped me learn that people relate to stories, so when they have something to grasp, it can be a truly magical result. This project wasn’t all smooth sailing. Working with this team challenged by brain, especially because they couldn’t get past the brainstorming activities and what the problem statement looked like. It was my challenge to translate the design thinking to a meaning they could understand. I revisited skills that I am currently learning at The Commons and shared those skills with my group.
